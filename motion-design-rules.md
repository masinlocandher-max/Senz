# Motion Design Rules

These rules are for future scroll-interactive animation work. They should be followed before redesigning the website.

## Motion Purpose

Animation should help visitors understand the archive. It should not be added just to look busy.

Good uses of motion:

- gently revealing archive sections as the visitor scrolls
- showing timeline progress
- helping cards feel organized and alive
- guiding attention to search, filters, and contribution actions
- creating a calm sense of memory, place, and discovery

Avoid:

- fast spinning effects
- flashing elements
- heavy effects that slow down phones
- animations that make text hard to read
- motion that feels like a game or advertisement

## Scroll Interaction Rules

- Start with subtle fade, rise, and parallax effects.
- Keep important text readable at all times.
- Do not make visitors wait for long animations before reading.
- Timeline animation should support the historical sequence.
- Archive cards can appear in small staggered groups.

## Accessibility Rules

- Respect reduced-motion settings.
- If a visitor prefers reduced motion, animations should be removed or greatly simplified.
- Do not use flashing or pulsing effects that may cause discomfort.

## Performance Rules

- Prefer CSS transitions and Intersection Observer JavaScript.
- Avoid large animation libraries unless clearly needed.
- Keep image and visual assets optimized.
- Test on mobile screen sizes before publishing.

## Content Safety Rule

Motion must not make uncertain content look more authoritative than it is. Items marked oral tradition, needs review, or research gap should remain clearly labeled.
